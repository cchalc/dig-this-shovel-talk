ROUND 1

The Central Question

Can XRF measure bucket grade accurately enough to justify $2.4M/year? XRF measures surfaces. Buckets contain heterogeneous volumes. The physics creates fundamental uncertainty.

XRF Physics: The Surface Problem

X-ray fluorescence penetrates only micrometers into rock surfaces. This is why conveyor belt analyzers use neutron-based (PGNAA) technology, not XRF.

🔬

What XRF Can Do

Detect copper at <10 ppm with modern SDD detectors. Classification accuracy of 75-93% reported in academic studies depending on cutoff grade.

⚠️

The Critical Limitation

Penetration depth: <1mm in copper sulfide ore. Thermo Fisher explicitly states surface technologies "may not be representative of entire material."

⚗️

Matrix Effects

Chalcopyrite (80% of deposit) contains 30.5% iron, which absorbs copper X-rays. Creates systematic measurement bias varying by mineralogy.

❓

No Independent Validation

All ShovelSense performance data originates from MineSense or their commercial partners. Zero peer-reviewed studies after 10+ years.

The Believer vs. The Skeptic

Two opposing positions on whether XRF adds signal or noise to the grade control process.

The Believer's Case

  • Counterfactual framing: Compare to blast holes (30-70% error), not perfect information
  • Timing advantage: Belt analyzers measure too late for truck diversion
  • Information fusion: Two imperfect signals beat one alone
  • Low threshold: Only 1.7% classification improvement needed for 2x ROI

The Skeptic's Case

  • Category error: A bucket has no single grade—only a distribution
  • Industry revealed preference: Billion-dollar plants use PGNAA, not XRF
  • Validation gap: No independent evidence after 10+ years
  • Noise risk: Low correlation = confident wrong answers
"Both monks assume the surface-volume correlation is fixed. But this correlation is measurable, spatially variable, and the critical unknown."
Round 1 Synthesis: Measure First

The surface-volume correlation is measurable, not assumed. Resolve the epistemic impasse before committing $2.4M/year.

What Gets Preserved

From Believer: Counterfactual framing (vs. blast holes, not perfect info). Timing advantage is real.

From Skeptic: Unverified claims shouldn't drive million-dollar decisions. Demand evidence.

What Gets Cancelled

From Believer: Assumption that sensor fusion automatically improves estimates.

From Skeptic: Assumption that low correlation makes XRF categorically worthless.

Round 1 Recommendation

Run a $150-300K measurement study before committing:

  • Measure classification accuracy at 0.32% Cu cutoff, stratified by zone
  • Pre-establish decision rules: >80% = deploy, 70-80% = analyze, <70% = reject

MRT Limitation: Bornite Blindness

Despite superior validation, MRT cannot detect bornite. Your 20% bornite content creates systematic measurement error.

What MRT Does Well

  • Peer-reviewed validation at Kansanshi (±0.028% Cu)
  • Volumetric measurement (no surface limitation)
  • Measures chalcopyrite specifically
  • Operational at 2,800+ t/h scale

The Bornite Problem

  • MRT tuned for specific mineral resonance
  • Bornite (Cu₅FeS₄) has different signature
  • Your 20% bornite = 20% copper invisible
  • Would systematically misclassify ore as waste

What This Means

MRT cannot be standalone technology for this deposit. PGNAA measures total copper regardless of mineralogy (chalcopyrite + bornite). The 20% bornite content makes PGNAA the better choice for ground truth, with MRT as potential supplement for high-throughput sorting in chalcopyrite-dominant zones.

Six Ways Deployments Fail

Technology works in isolation but fails at integration boundaries. These failure modes kill ROI regardless of technology choice.

1. Operator Bypass

Experienced operators override sensors based on intuition. Without trust-building, they find workarounds.

2. Integration Fragmentation

Sensors, dispatch, block models, mill systems rarely share data models. Which system wins when they disagree?

3. Calibration Decay

Without continuous recalibration, accuracy degrades over months. Mining into new zones invalidates old calibration.

4. Set-and-Forget Trap

Metrics tracked in Q1, then attention shifts. By month 18, nobody can prove ROI because measurement stopped.

5. Training Failure

Vendor training focuses on displays, not workflows. Operators don't know when to trust vs. override.

6. Metric Mismatch

Vendors optimize adoption (tonnage scanned). Mines need value (misclassification rate). Different goals, different outcomes.

FINAL SYNTHESIS

Don't Buy ShovelSense

Not because it doesn't work—it might. But because better-validated alternatives exist at 1/6th the cost, and your 20% bornite suggests surface-based XRF may also struggle.
