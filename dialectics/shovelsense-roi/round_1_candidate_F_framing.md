# Candidate F: Framing Dissolution — The Vendor's Binary

The entire debate accepts a premise that serves MineSense, not the mine: that the decision is whether to buy ShovelSense or not. Monk A argues for purchase despite imperfection. Monk B argues against purchase due to insufficient validation. Neither asks whether ShovelSense is the right technology to evaluate in the first place.

This is a fossil framing. The binary "buy ShovelSense / don't buy ShovelSense" arrives pre-packaged from MineSense's sales process. The vendor benefits when the customer's deliberation occurs entirely within this frame—even skepticism serves MineSense if it merely delays purchase rather than prompting evaluation of alternatives. The customer's actual question—"How do I improve grade control profitably at my operation?"—has been quietly replaced with the vendor's question: "Will you buy our product?"

## Who Benefits from This Frame

MineSense benefits in three ways. First, being the only solution under consideration means any comparison is against the status quo, not against competing approaches. The relevant benchmark becomes "better than nothing" rather than "better than alternatives." Second, the frame positions MineSense as synonymous with "shovel-based grade control," obscuring that other measurement points and technologies exist. Third, even extended evaluation cycles keep the customer engaged with MineSense rather than exploring the solution space.

The mine does not benefit from this frame. A copper porphyry operation seeking 2-4x ROI on $200K/month should be asking which grade control improvement offers the best return, not whether one particular vendor's product meets an arbitrary threshold.

## The Dissolution

The question is not "should I buy ShovelSense?" The question is "what is the most cost-effective way to reduce misclassification losses at my operation?" This reframing opens options that the vendor binary excluded.

### Alternative 1: Conveyor-Based Bulk Analysis (PGNAA/PFTNA)

Prompt Gamma Neutron Activation Analysis measures elemental composition of material on a moving belt. Unlike XRF, which measures only the surface, PGNAA penetrates the material stream and provides volumetric measurement. Systems from Scantech or Thermo Fisher can measure copper content continuously on the conveyor between pit and mill. The measurement point is later than the shovel, but the measurement quality may be higher. If the mine's misclassification problem occurs primarily at the coarse-reject decision (sending ore to waste or waste to mill), a belt analyzer catches errors before they reach the plant. Cost structures differ—capital versus operating lease—but the technology addresses the same economic problem through a different mechanism.

### Alternative 2: Magnetic Resonance Technology (MRT)

NextOre's MRT systems use bulk magnetic resonance to measure grade on conveyors. For copper sulphides, MRT can distinguish mineralized from barren material in real-time. Like PGNAA, this is a volumetric measurement that avoids XRF's surface-sampling limitation. MRT has seen deployment in copper operations and offers an alternative measurement physics entirely.

### Alternative 3: Improved Blast Hole Protocols

The cheapest intervention may require no new sensing technology. Faster lab turnaround on blast hole samples, tighter drill spacing in transition zones, or better geological modeling from existing data could reduce misclassification at lower cost than any sensor deployment. If the current blast hole sampling protocol leaves grade boundaries uncertain by ±5 meters, and ShovelSense's XRF validation is itself uncertain, the compounding uncertainty may make neither solution definitive. Investing in faster, denser, more accurate blast hole characterization attacks the problem at its source.

### Hybrid Approaches

A belt analyzer for reconciliation combined with improved blast hole sampling for dig-line decisions may outperform either approach alone. The belt analyzer provides ground truth for tuning blast hole interpretation; the improved blast holes provide routing decisions at the point where they matter. ShovelSense could fit into a hybrid strategy, but only after the mine understands what information gap it actually needs to close.

## The Reframed Questions

Instead of "should I buy ShovelSense," the customer should ask:

1. **Where in my material flow does misclassification actually occur, and what is each error's economic cost?**
2. **What information, measured where, would most reduce that cost?**
3. **What technologies can provide that information, at what price, with what validation evidence?**
4. **What is the ROI of each option, including non-sensor approaches like improved sampling?**

These questions serve the mine. The original binary served MineSense. The monks debated within a frame that was never the customer's to accept. The synthesis is not to resolve whether ShovelSense works well enough—it is to recognize that the question itself was imported from a party whose interests do not align with the decision-maker's, and to replace it with questions that do.
