# Monk B: The XRF Skeptic

## The Measurement Illusion: Why ShovelSense Cannot Deliver Value in Heterogeneous Copper Porphyry

### The Ontological Problem: A Bucket Has No Single Grade

A shovel bucket filled with 100 tonnes of heterogeneous copper porphyry ore does not possess "a grade." It possesses a three-dimensional distribution of copper concentrations—veins ranging from barren to 2.1% Cu, disseminated sulfides, fault gouge, and altered rock—all jumbled together in random orientation by blasting and loading.

XRF technology measures the surface of this distribution. The penetration depth of X-rays in copper sulfide ore is measured in micrometers—not millimeters, not centimeters. When MineSense claims to measure "bucket grade," they are measuring the elemental composition of exposed rock surfaces, then asserting that this surface sample predicts the volumetric average of the entire heterogeneous mass.

This is not a technological limitation that better sensors will overcome. This is a category error. The question is not whether XRF can detect copper—it can, reliably, down to parts-per-million concentrations. The question is whether the copper concentration on exposed rock surfaces correlates with the copper concentration inside that bucket.

In vein-controlled porphyry copper deposits, it does not.

The academic literature is unambiguous on this point. Es-sahly et al. (2025) achieved 92-93% classification accuracy for a sedimentary copper deposit in Morocco—and explicitly identified "heterogeneity error" as the most significant limitation. The MDPI study on sensor-based sorting states plainly: "XRF is widely used for surface analysis across industries, but its limited penetration depth presents challenges in mining applications, where surface measurements may not reflect volumetric grades."

The industry knows this. This is why every major bulk grade analyzer on conveyor belts uses neutron-based technology (PGNAA/PFTNA), not XRF. Neutrons penetrate the full depth of material on the belt. X-rays do not. Thermo Fisher, a leading instrumentation company, explicitly states: "Surface analysis technologies such as XRF... measure limited depths and surface areas that may not be representative of the entire amount of material on the belt."

## The Believer's Strongest Case

I acknowledge the appeal of the opposing position. The believer will argue:

First, that blast hole sampling is deeply flawed—and it is. The AusIMM Sampling 2008 conference documented that blast hole sampling "has acquired an extremely bad reputation for the last 50 years" due to delimitation biases, extraction losses, and weighting errors. Studies show errors of 30-70% in blast hole grade estimates.

Second, that ShovelSense adds information at the decision point—the moment of truck loading—when it matters most. Blast hole data is interpolated, aged, and smoothed. Real-time measurement, however imperfect, captures bucket-scale variability that block models miss.

Third, that MineSense reports impressive results: 5.9% of ore trucks diverted to waste, 7.2% of waste trucks diverted to ore, $2M+ revenue improvement in an 80-day case study. The believer will argue that imperfect measurement beats no measurement.

This argument has surface plausibility. It sounds reasonable. It is wrong.

---

## Why the Believer's Framing Is Wrong

The believer assumes that XRF measurement adds signal to the grade control process. But if the correlation between surface measurements and volumetric grade is low—and in heterogeneous, vein-controlled deposits, it is—then XRF adds noise, not signal.

Consider what happens when you make routing decisions based on noisy data. You have existing information: blast hole grades, geological models, geostatistical interpolation. This information has known limitations, but it represents genuine volumetric sampling. Now you overlay XRF surface measurements that may or may not correlate with volumetric grade.

If the XRF reading contradicts the blast hole model, which do you trust? If you trust the XRF—as ShovelSense is designed to encourage—and the XRF surface measurement does not represent volumetric grade, you have replaced a reasonable estimate with a confident error. You have sent ore to the waste dump because the exposed surfaces happened to be barren. You have sent waste to the crusher because the exposed surfaces happened to be rich.

The believer treats "some information" as always valuable. This is false. Unreliable information at the decision point can corrupt good information upstream. A confident wrong answer is worse than acknowledged uncertainty.

The research on porphyry copper sorting is explicit: "Some porphyry deposits have copper in very small grains that is very disseminated, with all of the rock containing small amounts of the metal but above the cut-off grade. With no barren rock to eliminate, the ore is not sortable." The same principle applies to bucket-scale measurement. If grade variability is primarily expressed in vein density and orientation rather than surface exposure, XRF measurement captures the wrong variable.

## The Verification Gap

Here is the deeper problem: every number supporting ShovelSense comes from MineSense or MineSense-commissioned sources.

The $2M+ revenue claim? MineSense press release.

The 4% recovery improvement? CIM Magazine, citing MineSense data.

The Whittle Consulting economic assessment? Commissioned by MineSense, modeling a "fictional but realistic mining operation," finding that "an idealized implementation of ShovelSense" offers 79% NPV improvement. An idealized implementation of a fictional mine is not evidence.

My web searches found no independent, third-party validation of ShovelSense technology. No peer-reviewed studies. No consulting firm evaluations that were not commissioned by MineSense. No before/after comparisons conducted by mining companies themselves and published independently.

For a technology that has been deployed at "nine copper mines" over multiple years, the absence of independent validation is not neutral—it is damning. If ShovelSense delivered the claimed ROI, mines would publish their results. Equipment vendors would cite customer data. Academic researchers would study the implementations. The silence speaks.

## The Strongest Case Against

If ShovelSense reliably delivered 4x ROI, it would be standard equipment at every copper mine in the world. It is not.

If the technology worked as claimed, there would be peer-reviewed validation studies from multiple independent research groups. There are not.

If XRF surface measurement predicted volumetric grade in heterogeneous ore, the industry would use XRF for bulk conveyor analysis. Instead, the industry spent decades developing and deploying neutron-based analyzers specifically because XRF does not work for bulk measurement.

MineSense has been operating since 2013. They have deployed at multiple mines across multiple years. The claimed economics are extraordinary: $9M annualized revenue improvement against $2.4M annual cost. If these numbers were real and verifiable, capital markets would have noticed. Strategic acquirers would have noticed. The mining industry would have noticed.

The gap between marketing claims and independent evidence is not a gap that more time will close. It is the evidence.

## The Economic Calculation

Let us work through the math that MineSense does not show you.

At a 0.32% Cu cutoff grade in a heterogeneous deposit, truck loads near cutoff could genuinely be ore or waste depending on which veins were captured in the bucket. The within-bucket coefficient of variation is likely 40-80% based on fractal grade distribution studies. Many buckets are genuinely ambiguous.

Now consider misclassification rates. If ShovelSense has an 85% accuracy rate—generous given the surface-volume correlation problem—that means 15% of redirected trucks are wrong. Of the claimed 5.9% ore-to-waste diversion, 15% are ore sent to the waste dump. Of the claimed 7.2% waste-to-ore diversion, 15% are waste sent to the crusher.

The asymmetry matters. Sending ore to waste loses the recoverable metal value. Sending waste to ore costs processing and dilutes concentrate grade. At copper prices of $4/lb and processing costs of $15/tonne, the math is unforgiving.

If the actual accuracy is 75%—which multiple academic studies suggest is realistic for heterogeneous ore—then 25% of all ShovelSense redirections are errors. At that point, the expected value calculation turns negative. You are paying $200K/month to make your grade control worse.

And this assumes the 85-75% accuracy range is correct. We do not know because MineSense has not submitted to independent validation. The accuracy could be 60%. It could be 50%. We are asked to trust marketing claims for a $2.4M annual expenditure.

---

## Conclusion

The customer asking this question has a 0.32% Cu cutoff in a heterogeneous copper porphyry deposit with vein-controlled mineralization. The exposed surfaces of a bucket load do not predict the volumetric grade. XRF measurement adds noise, not signal. The claimed benefits are unverifiable vendor marketing. The industry standard for bulk grade measurement deliberately avoids XRF's surface limitation.

For $200,000 per month, this customer deserves auditable evidence. It does not exist. The investment is unjustified.
